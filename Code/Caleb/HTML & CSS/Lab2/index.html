Wood Glue Squeeze Out Clean-up: Methodical Patience & Restraint

One of the many things all woodworkers experience is the conundrum of how to approach the clean-up of glue squeeze-out after a glue-up. For those woodworkers not using wood glues, in particular the water-solubl“yellow wood glue” kind such as Titebond, this may not apply, but I doubt any conventions apply to these estranged woodworkers from the rest of us joiners utilizing the superb strength & reliability of water-solublaliphatic resin emulsion glues. Now, many of the woodworkers I subscribe to would argue that the discussion should begin with the unnecessary nature of the existence of squeeze-out to begin with. They would discussqueeze-out prevention via either taping off your joints with painters’ tape (or in my case – special sign/print industry 3M light green tape). A seasoned woodworker would argue they never get any squeeze out becausthey know just the perfect amount of glue to apply to a joint. It’s no irony that the notorious Wood Whisperer, Marc Spagnuolo, fielded a question and gave a lengthy quality response this on very topic on his firs“Wood Talk” podcast in April of 20071. Long chisel, short, scraper, the opinions on how to approach wood glue squeeze-out like most woodworking topics, is hotly debated. In this post, I’ll regurgitate what little have learned in my readings online, as well as some brief anecdotes of various specific methods & tools I have encountered personally in my squeeze-out clean-ups.

Like much of what I know, it all begins with Mr. Ken Vetterik (https://www.facebook.com/Ken-Vetterick-Woodworking-608764679241036/), who I first met through my father & began assisting him in his wood shop & loadinunloading his kilns & assisting with moving his slabs & lumber. I say this endearingly, as Ken is to this day one of the most proficient woodworkers, not just in his time, planning, processing, but just in nearly moseverything the man does. Let’s label his approach as the “Norm Abrams” method. This method is essentially the wet rag, sand, and “I’m so damn good, my joints seldom have squeeze-out” process. Now taking the time tlay down tape, or pre-finish, is typically not ever in the plan for Ken. He just doesn’t have time for it. His final finish was usually a wipe-on oil-based finish, and so he just made sure to fully wipe thsqueeze-out away with a clean damp rag, and made sure to sand it afterwards.

After hearing Marc’s approach, which is very similar to my current day approach, I feel reassured in the hybrid, measured & patient approach he described, and I employ. Let’s call this method, the “wait and scrape thskinned squeeze-out with a proper tool (special chisels, cut plastic straws, wood edged tools, plane irons, and cabinet scrapers) and then sand”. Like much of woodworking, I firmly believe it takes a steady, measured proper touch to deal with wood glue squeeze-out properly. It takes patience & restraint.

Sanding & Mindfulness

What must be said about why mindfulness is essential to fine woodworking in general, but especially sanding.

Too often every woodworker, myself included, loses their focus. This seldom happens with a power tool, especially saws, as these incidents more often than not end up in ER visits and ruined pieces. However, sanding isoften a process most woodworkers disdain and forget to maintain their focus.

Hence, like a yogi mindful of their breath, body, muscles being engaged, and joints being stretched – a woodworker must be mindful while sanding.

A mindful woodworker will pay attention to:

  • - Keeping the sander/sanding block/pad flat or at whichever specific angle you are attempting to sand
  • - Sand paper is essentially a pad of tiny blades, the abrasives in sand paper will cut, round, and remove material when applied to wood. This may seem obvious, but I always am fascinated by imagining what sand paperwould look like under a magnifying glass whilst sanding.
  • - Sand with the grain
  • - Move deliberately, especially when using power sanders – keep the sander moving

The list could go on. Sanding, much like all of woodworking, is the ultimate exercise in focus, precision, art, movement, and darn good ole fun.
